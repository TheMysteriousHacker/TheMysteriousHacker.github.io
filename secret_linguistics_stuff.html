Introduction

Dialects
A dialect continuum is a range of dialects spoken across a continuous geographic space. Neighbouring dialects are quite similar to each other, but dialects on opposite ends might not be mutually intelligible. Two dialects are considered to be mutually intelligible when a speaker of one dialect can (by and large) understand the other and vice versa.
  • A native speaker speaker of a language is a speaker who acquired the language as a child from a caregiver in a natural setting
  • A sign is a discrete unit of meaning.
  • A conventional sign is a sign that all members of a language community (e.g., all English speakers) agree to use with a certain meaning.
  • onomatopoeia: When a word sounds like the thing it means.
  • Linguistic competence contains the knowledge of:
    • The sounds of the language:
      • Which sounds can be used (phonetics)
      • How the sounds pattern (phonology)
    • The words of the language:
      • The arbitrary relationship between certain sequences of sounds and particular concepts or meanings.
      • How words are formed from smaller units of meaning (morphology)
    • How words combine to form sentences (syntax)
    • How meaning is derived (semantics)
  • A speaker's linguistic performance is their actual use of language in real situations.
  • Linguists call "the mental system that allows human beings to form and interpret sounds, words, and sentences of their language" a grammar.
linguistic competence.
The ability that a speaker of a language has to produce and perceive novel words and sentences, and at the same time to recognize the string of sounds and string of words that are not acceptable words and sentences of that language, arises from a special type of knowledge that linguists call linguistic competence.
Linguistic competence is the unconscious knowledge a speaker has of his or her native language.

This knowledge is the grammar that the speaker acquires and internalizes as a child, also called aspeaker's mental grammar or I-language.

Morphology

  • Morphology is the study of the structure and formation of words.
  • Words are made up of morphemes:
    • The minimal unit of meaning.
    • The smallest chunk of language that carries information about content or grammatical function.
  • Lexicon: Mental dictionary



  • Endocentric: Compounds with an internal head word from which the compound draws its core meaning and grammatical category; the non-head word modifies the head.
  • Exocentric: Compounds with no internal head that have an idiosyncratic meaning
Language Classification
An analytic language is one in which almost every word in the language is monomorphemic and there is a tendency to avoid affixes, especially inflectional affixes. Grammatical information is expressed with free morphemes. A synthetic-agglutinative language is one in which words are typically formed by agglutination, the process of stringing together clearly identifiable morphemes, each with a singular meaning. A synthetic-fusional language is one in which words are formed by combinine multiple morphemes but a single morpheme is often a combination of multiple pieces of grammatical information fused together. A polysynthetic language is a highly synthetic language with a high degree of affixation and fusion of morphemes. Words can be made up of multiple stems without compounding, through a process of incorporating subject and object nouns into the verb.

Syntax

  • Syntax is the study of sentence construction




  • A Phrase Structure Grammar consists of rules that generate sentences:
    • Phrase Structure Rules: Describe our observations about the distribution of phrasal categories.
    • Lexical Insertion Rules: Describe our observations about the syntactic category of each word.





  • The head of a phrase is the word around which the phrase is built.
  • The complement(s) of a head is the constituent(s) which provides information about entities or locations implied by the meaning of the head.





  • Constituency Tests:
    • Topicalization test: A string of words is a constituent if it can be spoken at the front of the sentence while preserving the acceptability of the sentence.
    • Question and Answer test: A string of words is a constituent if you can replace it with a question word (e.g., who, what, when, where), forming a question, and the string of words is an acceptable answer to the question.
    • Coordination test: A string of words is a constituent if it can be coordinated with another string of words that is a constituent.
    • Clefting: A string of words is a constituent if it can be inserted into the initial part of the cleft, with the remainder of the sentence in the final part of the cleft.





  • Deep structure: The representation of a sentence afeter Phrase Structure Rules has been applied.
  • Surface Structure: The representation of a sentence after Transformational Rules have applied.
  • Two Transformational rules:
    • Aux- movement:
      1. Find a constituent with the label Aux.
      2. Make a copy of this Aux constituent at the fron of the tree.
      3. Apply the rule CP -> CS to the top S of the tree.
      4. Attach the copy of the Aux to the tree by inserting it in C.
      5. Cross-out the lexical items in the original Aux.
    • Wh- movement:
      1. Find a constituent with the label NP.
      2. Make a copy of this NP constituent at the fron of the tree.
      3. Attach the copy of the NP to the tree by inserting it into CP (as a sister to CP).
      4. Cross-out the lexical items in the original NP.

Semantics

  • Semantics is the study of the meaning of linguistic expressions, like morphemes, words, and phrases, and how meanings are combined in complex expressions.
  • Pragmatics is the study of how meaning depends on the context of utterance and the knowledge and intentions of the speaker and listener.


  • What does it mean for a word to mean something?
    • Option 1: Language connects with concepts and ideas in the mind (cognative approach).
    • Option 2: Language connects with the world (referential approach).
  • Denotation: What an expression denotes is what it applies to in the world.
  • Connotation: Necessary and sufficient properties that a term poses which determine its denotation.


  • Anaphoric expressions are expressions which cannot be assigned a reference without knowledge of some aspect of a sentence's context-of-utterance (which has two components: cotext and setting).
  • An anaphor which depends on some aspect of the setting is called an exophor and if it depends on some aspect of the cotext is called an endophor.
  • A linguistic device which depends on some aspect of the cotext for its meaning is called an endophor. Sometimes the device is an overt expression (a proform), and sometimes the device is the absence of an expression (ellipsis).


  • A set is an abstract collection of objects, and the objects that are collected in a set are called its members.
Inferential Relations
Entailment Presuppositions Implicatures
Sentence A entails sentence B just if B is true whenever A is true and A cannot be true without B being true as well. Presuppositions are similar to entailments in that they cannot be cancelled. However, they display different behaviour; they are preserved under negation (adding not) and question formation. An implicature is an inference that arises as a result of enriching the literal meaning of a sentence with shared assumptions and general reasoning. The reasoning typically involves reference to conversational maxims.

Phonetics

  • Phonetics, from the Greek phōnē, meaning sound or to speak, is the study of producing and perceiving speech sounds.
    • Articulatory phonetics is the study of how the human vocal tract produces sounds.
    • Acoustic phonetics is the study of the physical properties of speech sounds.
    • Auditory phonetics is the study of the way listeners perceive speech sounds.

Phonology

  • Phonology is the study of how speech sounds form systems and patterns in particular languages.
  • A syllable (σ) is a mental grouping of a set of sound segments into a unit



  • Phonemes, written in / /, exist in the mind of a speaker as distinct sounds.
  • Phonemes often have more than one phonetic variant, or allophone. Which variant is used is determined by their environment.
  • The different phonetic variants (or pronunciations) of a morpheme are called allomorphs.



  • Phonological Features (also called distinctive features) are the most basic unit of phonologicalstructure.

  • Jargon is specialized, often technical, language used by a particular group or profession.
